## imo-2026-01

- Distinct openings:
  1. **Per-prime coordinatewise decomposition (strongest lead).** Since `v_p(gcd(m,n)) = min(v_p(m),v_p(n))` and `v_p(lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n)) = max(v_p(m),v_p(n)) - min(v_p(m),v_p(n))`, the whole board move acts *independently, prime by prime*, on the exponent-vector `(v_p(x_1),...,v_p(x_2026))` for each prime `p`: a move at positions `(i,j)` replaces the pair `(a,b)=(v_p(x_i),v_p(x_j))` with `(min(a,b), max(a,b)-min(a,b))`. This is the subtractive Euclidean algorithm acting on that pair, done simultaneously for all `p`. This decomposition is the natural top-level target: prove two facts about this per-prime pairwise transform and the whole problem falls out.
  2. **Termination via a lexicographic monovariant on (count-of-entries->1, total Ω).** Two disjoint effects per move: if `gcd(m,n)=1`, then the move replaces `{m,n}` with `{1, mn}` — the count of entries `>1` strictly drops by 1 (a genuine "wasted" move that just removes a slot from play). If `gcd(m,n)>1`, the count of entries `>1` is unchanged, but for every prime `p | gcd(m,n)`, the pair-sum `a+b` strictly drops to `max(a,b)<a+b` (both `a,b>0`), so `Ω(m)+Ω(n) = Σ_p a+b` strictly decreases. So `(#\{i: x_i>1\}, Σ_i Ω(x_i))` decreases lexicographically every single move, both coordinates bounded below by 0 (in fact `Ω`-sum can't go below 0 and count can't go below 0), forcing termination in finitely many moves. This looks like a clean, complete answer to part (a) — much simpler than needing a strictly-decreasing scalar.
  3. **Invariant for part (b): per-prime gcd-of-exponents.** For each prime `p`, define `G_p = gcd(v_p(x_1), ..., v_p(x_2026))` over the *initial* board (using `gcd(0,e)=e`, `gcd(0,0)=0`). Claim `G_p` is an exact invariant of the whole process: a move only changes the pair `(a,b)` at two coordinates to `(min(a,b), max(a,b)-min(a,b))`, and `gcd(min(a,b), max(a,b)-min(a,b)) = gcd(a,b)` by the standard Euclidean identity (checked also at the boundary `a=0` or `b=0`). Since `gcd` of a multiset is associative/decomposable (`gcd(all) = gcd(gcd(a,b), rest)`), and only the pair `(a,b)` at the chosen coordinates changes while its gcd is preserved, `G_p` is untouched by any move, for every prime `p`, at every step. At the terminal state exactly one entry `M>1` remains and all others equal `1`; then `gcd` of the exponent multiset at prime `p` (all zero except possibly `v_p(M)`) equals `v_p(M)` exactly. Hence `v_p(M) = G_p` is *forced* for every prime `p`, independent of how the moves were made — giving `M = ∏_p p^{G_p}` uniquely. This closes part (b) directly, and as a bonus reconfirms `M>1`: since the initial numbers are all `>1`, some prime `p` divides some `x_i`; letting `e=v_p(x_i)>0`, if `p` divides no other `x_j` then `G_p = gcd(e,0,0,...) = e > 0`, so `M` is forced `>1`. (If `p` divides several `x_j`, `G_p` could a priori be smaller but is still well-defined; a fully rigorous argument needs to confirm at least one prime ends with `G_p>0`, which follows since `Ω`-sum over the whole board only ever decreases and the process can't wipe out every prime's contribution simultaneously to 0 unless all numbers were already 1 — worth double-checking in the outline, but strongly indicated.)
  4. (Weaker/rejected opening, kept as a foil) Naive guesses for `M` — e.g. "product of all numbers" (false: product strictly changes, `gcd(m,n)·lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n) = lcm(m,n) ≤ mn`, generally `<`) or "lcm of all initial numbers" (false, see numerics below) — both fail numerically; do not pursue.

- Small-case / intuition notes (numerically verified via `python3`/`sympy` random simulation, `/tmp/sim.py`, `/tmp/sim2.py` — evidence/conjecture-then-checked-against-derivation, not yet a full proof):
  - Randomized play (many random pairings) on random small boards (size 3–10, entries up to 80) always terminates with exactly one entry `>1`, consistent with part (a).
  - For each fixed initial board, repeated random play from that board always converges to the *same* terminal value `M`, consistent with part (b) (invariance under choices).
  - The naive guess `M = lcm(x_1,...,x_n) = ∏_p p^{max_i v_p(x_i)}` is **wrong**: e.g. board `[10,36,4]` gives simulated `M=90` vs `lcm=180` (ratio 2); board `[38,56,53,50]` gives `M=352450` vs `lcm=1409800` (ratio 4); ratios of `lcm`/`M` observed were always positive integers (1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, 18, 20, 36, 120, 180, ...) across 30+ trials, i.e. `M | lcm` always, strictly less whenever some prime has multiple board entries sharing it with non-multiple-of-each-other exponents.
  - The formula `M = ∏_p p^{G_p}` with `G_p = gcd(v_p(x_1),...,v_p(x_n))` (extending the gcd over the whole board, using `gcd(0,e)=e`) matches the simulated terminal `M` **exactly in all 30 independently-seeded random trials** (`/tmp/sim2.py`), including boards of size up to 10 and values up to 80, with 6 independent random playouts per board all agreeing. This is strong numerical support for the closed form derived analytically in opening 3 above.
