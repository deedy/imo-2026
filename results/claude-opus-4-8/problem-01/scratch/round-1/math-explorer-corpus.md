## imo-2026-01

- Distinct openings (retrieval/analogy lens):
  1. **Per-prime Euclidean reduction (the load-bearing structural fact).** Fix a prime p and look at the vector of p-adic valuations (v_p(n_1),…,v_p(n_2026)). A move on entries i,j replaces the pair (a,b)=(v_p(n_i),v_p(n_j)) with (min(a,b), max(a,b)−min(a,b)) — exactly one step of the subtractive Euclidean algorithm on that coordinate pair, and the OTHER 2024 coordinates are untouched. This decouples the problem into 2026-vectors, one per prime, each evolving by an independent pairwise "Euclid-swap." This is the natural way in to both (a) (termination) and (b) (uniqueness of M), and every genuinely different opening below is really a different way to analyze this same per-prime vector process.
  2. **Invariant opening for part (b):** gcd(min(a,b), max(a,b)−min(a,b)) = gcd(a,b) (standard Euclidean-algorithm gcd invariance). So for each prime p, g_p := gcd(v_p(n_1),…,v_p(n_2026)) is preserved by every move, from the start to the very end. Combined with (a) — that the terminal state has all valuations 0 except a single position holding M — gcd(0,…,0,v_p(M)) = v_p(M) must equal g_p. Hence, GIVEN (a), M = ∏_p p^{g_p} is forced and manifestly independent of the choices. This reduces (b) to a one-line consequence of (a) plus the Euclidean gcd invariance fact — the real work is entirely in (a).
  3. **Monovariant opening for part (a) (termination):** need a quantity that strictly decreases (or increases toward a bound) with every move, on the FULL 2026-tuple of integers (not per-prime), to show the process halts. Candidates: total sum Σn_i (does it strictly decrease? gcd(m,n)+lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n) vs m+n — need to check: this is NOT always strictly decreasing, e.g. m=n gives gcd=m, lcm/gcd=1, so pair (m,m)→(m,1), sum drops from 2m to m+1, decreases when m>1. But general coprime m,n>1: gcd=1, lcm/gcd=mn, pair→(1,mn), sum m+n → 1+mn, which is an INCREASE for m,n≥2. So naive sum is not monotone — must instead work per-prime, e.g. via Σ v_p(n_i)^2 or the Euclidean algorithm's own decrease certificate (sum of the pair strictly decreases unless min=0, i.e. unless the pair is already "resolved").
  4. **Product-of-factorial-type or count-of-nonzero-valuations monovariant:** for each prime p, track the number of indices i with v_p(n_i) > 0. A per-prime Euclid-swap step on (a,b) with a,b>0 either keeps both nonzero or drives one to 0 (when a=b, or after finitely many swaps by standard Euclidean algorithm termination). Need an argument that bounds the number of "swaps that keep both coordinates positive" — this is exactly bounded by the Euclidean algorithm's step count on (a,b), which is finite and controllable (e.g. by Fibonacci/log bound), but moves are also interleaved across different index pairs, so a global monovariant across the whole tuple is needed, not just per-pair.
  5. **Reformulate as many independent "chip games," one per prime, each a Euclidean-algorithm race**: this suggests bounding total termination via Σ_p (finite bound on Euclidean steps for the p-coordinate vector), summed over the finitely many primes appearing — since only finitely many primes divide any of the initial 2026 numbers, and each per-prime sub-process is itself a finite composition of independent pairwise Euclidean reductions.

- Candidate technique(s): Invariants & monovariants (KB "Invariants & monovariants", "Invariant/monovariant" general method); p-adic valuation decomposition (unique factorization); the classical Euclidean algorithm gcd-invariance and termination argument, applied coordinatewise per prime.

- Cheap-kill candidates: 
  - Check whether Σn_i itself is monotone — NOT always (see opening 3): coprime pairs increase the sum, so a naive "sum decreases" argument is a dead end / needs replacing by a per-prime valuation-based quantity.
  - Check number of entries >1 is non-increasing: after a move, gcd(m,n)=1 is possible (if m,n coprime) turning one of the two into 1 — so the move can only ever keep the count of entries >1 the same or decrease it by exactly 1 (never increase), since at least one of gcd, lcm/gcd is >1 unless coprime, in which case one output is 1. This immediately gives: the count of entries >1 is non-increasing across all moves — cheap structural fact worth confirming rigorously and using for part (a)'s "at most one remains" framing, combined with a monovariant to guarantee it doesn't get permanently stuck with ≥2 entries >1.

- Analogous past problems (cruxes) — filtered domain=number_theory & combinatorics, subtopics invariants-and-monovariants / p-adic-valuation / processes-and-algorithms / divisibility-and-gcd, then judged for genuine analogy:
  1. **aimo-0028** (number_theory, divisibility-and-gcd) — "Strip an even factor from a gcd of two near-equal quadratics via the Euclidean step to prove successive value-prime-divisors are distinct." Uses gcd(a,b)=gcd(a−b,b) Euclidean-step reasoning, same algebraic identity family as our per-prime reduction (gcd(m,n) is unchanged by replacing one argument with a combination of the two) — a genuine analogy for justifying the gcd-invariance claim in opening 2, though the problem itself (largest prime factor of n^4+n^2+1) is not close in surface form.
  2. **aimo-0836** (combinatorics, invariants-and-monovariants / induction-and-construction) — board of integers, move erases two numbers a,b and writes {a+b,|a−b|} (unless already present). This is the closest STRUCTURAL analogue found: it's literally a "blackboard, pick two numbers, replace by a Euclidean-flavor pair" process, and its crux moves are about a "doubling primitive" (apply the move twice to scale a pair by 2) and a minimal-sum-counterexample descent to show the board count shrinks. The 2026-integers problem's per-move replacement (gcd, lcm/gcd) is multiplicative rather than additive, but the "two numbers replaced by a function-pair of themselves, iterate until stuck" shape and the "argue via a minimal/extremal configuration" descent technique are directly reusable framings — worth the outliner's attention as a possible ALTERNATIVE termination argument (contradiction on a minimal total sum/product counterexample) instead of a direct monovariant.
  3. **aimo-0943** (number_theory, p-adic-valuation) — "Use the exponent in the 2-adic decomposition as a strict monovariant... exponent drops by exactly one each step, forcing termination." This is the cleanest template for a termination proof VIA a p-adic-valuation monovariant that decreases by a controlled amount per relevant step — directly the shape opening 3/4 wants, though the recurrence structure differs (single sequence vs. pairwise board moves). Confirms that "isolate one prime, track its valuation profile, show it's forced toward a fixed point" is a standard, provably-terminating technique in this corpus.
  - Also noted but less analogous: aimo-0324 (squarefree-part monovariant, a game not a closure process) and aimo-0917 (blackboard game with x+y/|x−y| moves under adversarial play — has a genuinely different two-player structure, not directly transferable since our problem has NO adversary, just "continues while possible," but its "board entries stay powers of 2 under induction" style invariant is a reminder that induction on structural form (not just size) can also pin down intermediate states).

- Small-case / intuition notes (numerically verified, hence CONJECTURE-CONFIRMED not proved): simulated the process in Python (random pairing, 20 trials, 6 numbers each, values up to 60) and in every trial the process terminated with exactly one integer M>1 remaining, and M exactly equaled ∏_p p^{gcd_i v_p(n_i)} — i.e. M = the unique integer with v_p(M) = gcd(v_p(n_1),…,v_p(n_k)) for every prime p. This strongly supports openings 1–2 as the right characterization of M for part (b), and confirms the "count of entries >1 never increases" cheap-kill fact held in every trial (final state always had exactly one entry >1, never more, regardless of pairing order). This is empirical evidence only — the outliner still needs a rigorous termination proof (part a) and the clean write-up of the gcd-invariance argument (part b).
