## imo-2026-01

- Distinct openings:
  1. **Per-prime exponent-gcd invariant (the strong opening — essentially solves both parts at once).** For a fixed prime p, write a=v_p(m), b=v_p(n) for the two chosen board numbers. Then v_p(gcd(m,n))=min(a,b) and v_p(lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n))=max(a,b)-min(a,b)=|a-b|. So on the vector of p-exponents across the 2026 positions, a move replaces (a,b) at the two chosen positions by (min(a,b),|a-b|), leaving all other positions' p-exponents untouched. Using the classical identity gcd(a,b)=gcd(min(a,b),|a-b|) (a single step of the subtractive Euclidean algorithm) together with associativity of gcd across a multiset, the **gcd of ALL 2026 exponents of p** (call it g_p) is exactly preserved by every move, for every prime p, unconditionally — regardless of which two positions are chosen and regardless of whether the two chosen numbers m,n are even divisible by p. This is a genuine invariant (not just monovariant), and it does NOT require primes to decouple across a single move (a move updates all primes at the chosen pair simultaneously) — the invariance holds prime-by-prime independently of what happens to the other primes.
  2. **Product monovariant + count-of-(>1)-entries monovariant, for termination.** Total product P = ∏(board numbers) satisfies P_new/P_old = lcm(m,n)/(mn) = 1/gcd(m,n) ≤ 1, strict iff gcd(m,n)>1. The count N of board entries >1 never increases and strictly decreases whenever gcd(m,n)=1 (a coprime pair m,n>1 becomes {1, mn}: N drops by exactly 1). The lexicographic pair (N,P) strictly decreases every move (in case gcd(m,n)=1, N drops; in case gcd(m,n)>1, N is unchanged or drops, and whenever N is unchanged P strictly drops) — a well-founded quantity in ℕ×ℕ, giving termination directly (no need for anything fancier). This is the natural finiteness argument, orthogonal to opening 1 but combines with it below.
  3. Combine 1+2 to finish (a) cleanly: termination gives a final state with N∈{0,1} (moves stop exactly when at most one entry is >1). Rule out N=0: if all entries became 1, every exponent of every prime would be 0 at the end, so g_p (invariant, by opening 1) would be 0 for every prime p — but g_p = gcd of the INITIAL exponents of p, and since the board starts with 2026 integers >1, some initial number has v_p(that number)>0 for some prime p dividing it, and gcd of a multiset containing a positive value is a positive integer (gcd(0,...,0,e>0,...)=e>0), so g_p>0 for that p — contradiction. Hence N=1 always, proving (a) completely (termination + exactly one survivor).
  4. Part (b) then falls out immediately from opening 1: at the terminal state only M is >1, all others are 1 (all exponents 0), so v_p(M) = gcd(0,0,...,0,v_p(M)) = the invariant g_p = gcd of the INITIAL exponents of p across all 2026 starting numbers, for every prime p. This pins down M = ∏_p p^{g_p} completely in terms of the initial data, independent of Confucius's choices — done.

- Candidate technique(s): p-adic valuation / per-prime exponent tracking; the classical Euclidean-algorithm gcd identity gcd(a,b)=gcd(min(a,b),|a-b|); invariant vs. monovariant separation (invariant g_p pins the value, monovariant (N,P) forces termination and rules out the degenerate N=0 outcome).

- Cheap-kill candidates: None needed beyond the above — the per-prime gcd identity is itself an extremely cheap, fully elementary "kill" of the whole problem (no heavy machinery). One thing to flag as a **subtlety, not a dead end**: it's tempting to think primes "decouple" so you could treat each prime totally independently including which positions get chosen — this is FALSE in the sense that the actual sequence of moves (which two positions get combined) is a single shared random/adversarial choice across all primes simultaneously (a move touches all of m's and n's prime-exponents at once). What DOES decouple is only the *transformation rule*: for each fixed prime, the exponent pair (a,b) at the two touched positions updates to (min,|a-b|) using only that prime's own exponents, regardless of the other primes' exponents at those positions — so the gcd-invariance argument (opening 1) can be run one prime at a time even though the position-selection process itself is shared/coupled. Do not let the outliner claim primes are "independent processes" — only claim the per-move update rule is prime-local.

- Small-case / intuition notes: Verified numerically (Python, `math.gcd`/`lcm`, random move order, random starting multisets of size 3–8 with entries in [2,50], 20 trials, seed=1) that (i) the process always terminates, (ii) always leaves exactly one entry >1, and (iii) that entry always equals M = ∏_p p^{gcd of initial p-exponents across the whole starting multiset} — matching prediction in all 20/20 trials regardless of move order. This is strong (though non-proof) confirmation that opening 1+3+4 above is the actual intended solution and gives the correct, order-independent value of M.
