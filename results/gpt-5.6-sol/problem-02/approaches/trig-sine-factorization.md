## Current best
Orient the plane with \(C\) left of \(AB\), and put \(a=BC,b=CA,c=AB\), \(\alpha=\angle A,\beta=\angle B,\gamma=\angle C\), and
\[
x=\angle KBA=\angle ACL,\quad y=\angle LBK=\angle LNC,
\quad z=\angle LCK=\angle BMK.
\]
The interior hypotheses give the strict ray orders
\[
BA,BK,BL,BC\quad\text{and}\quad CA,CL,CK,CB,
\]
so
\[
0<x<\min(\beta,\gamma),\quad0<y<\beta-x,\quad0<z<\gamma-x. \tag{1}
\]
Indeed, the two triangle-interiority conditions put the rays strictly inside the vertex angles, while “\(K\) inside \(\angle LBA\)” and “\(L\) inside \(\angle ACK\)” fix their orders. Thus no reflected branch is discarded.

By the **Sine Law** (Synthetic toolkit in the knowledge base), triangles \(BMK,BCK\) give
\[
\boxed{\frac c2\frac{\sin z}{\sin(x+z)}
=a\frac{\sin(\gamma-x-z)}{\sin(\alpha+2x+z)}}. \tag{2}
\]
Triangles \(CNL,BCL\) similarly give
\[
\boxed{\frac b2\frac{\sin y}{\sin(x+y)}
=a\frac{\sin(\beta-x-y)}{\sin(\alpha+2x+y)}}. \tag{3}
\]
For example, the angles of \(BCK\) are \(\beta-x,\gamma-x-z,\alpha+2x+z\), and those of \(BCL\) are \(\beta-x-y,\gamma-x,\alpha+2x+y\). Every denominator is the sine of an angle of one of these nondegenerate triangles, hence is nonzero.

Place
\[
A=(0,0),\ B=(c,0),\ C=(b\cos\alpha,b\sin\alpha),
\quad t=\frac c2\frac{\sin z}{\sin(x+z)},\quad
s=\frac b2\frac{\sin y}{\sin(x+y)}.
\]
The ray orders give
\[
K=(X,Y)=(c-t\cos x,t\sin x), \tag{4}
\]
\[
L=(U,V)=(b\cos\alpha-s\cos(\alpha+x),
 b\sin\alpha-s\sin(\alpha+x)). \tag{5}
\]
The minus sign in (5) is forced because \(\overrightarrow{CL}\) points opposite to direction \(\alpha+x\). Put \(\Delta=XV-YU\ne0\), since \(A,K,L\) are noncollinear.

The circle \(\omega=(AKL)\) has equation
\(q_1^2+q_2^2-w_1q_1-w_2q_2=0\). Substitution of (4),(5) and Cramer's rule give
\[
 w_1=\frac{(X^2+Y^2)V-(U^2+V^2)Y}{\Delta},\quad
 w_2=\frac{X(U^2+V^2)-U(X^2+Y^2)}{\Delta}. \tag{6}
\]
If the oriented lines \(AB,AC\) meet \(\omega\) again at \(D=pB,E=qC\), direct substitution gives the explicit signed formulas
\[
\boxed{p=\frac{w_1}{c}},\qquad
\boxed{q=\frac{w_1\cos\alpha+w_2\sin\alpha}{b}}. \tag{7}
\]
The real parameters cover intersections on extensions as well as segments.

It remains to derive from (2),(3) the explicit identity
\[
\begin{aligned}
0={}&(c^2-b^2)\Delta
-2c\{(X^2+Y^2)V-(U^2+V^2)Y\}\\
&+2b\cos\alpha\{(X^2+Y^2)V-(U^2+V^2)Y\}\\
&+2b\sin\alpha\{X(U^2+V^2)-U(X^2+Y^2)\}. \tag{8}
\end{aligned}
\]
This is a determinate trigonometric factorization. An exponential-polynomial check confirms that (8) is in the ideal generated by the cross-multiplied residuals
\[
R_z=c\sin z\sin(\alpha+2x+z)-2a\sin(x+z)\sin(\gamma-x-z), \tag{9}
\]
\[
R_y=b\sin y\sin(\alpha+2x+y)-2a\sin(x+y)\sin(\beta-x-y), \tag{10}
\]
after using \(a:b:c=\sin\alpha:\sin\beta:\sin\gamma\). I did not obtain a sufficiently short displayed decomposition \(\mathrm{LHS}(8)=P R_z+Q R_y\) whose verification is human-checkable. This is the sole open gap; the computational check is not asserted as proof.

Once (8) is supplied, division by \(\Delta\) and (6),(7) yields
\[
c^2(1-2p)=b^2(1-2q). \tag{11}
\]
The final directed-power step is rigorous. Orient \(AB\) from \(A\) to \(B\). Since \(M=B/2\),
\(\overline{MA}=-c/2\) and \(\overline{MD}=c(p-1/2)\). By the directed **power-of-a-point theorem**,
\[
\operatorname{Pow}_{\omega}(M)=\frac{c^2}{4}(1-2p). \tag{12}
\]
Similarly,
\[
\operatorname{Pow}_{\omega}(N)=\frac{b^2}{4}(1-2q). \tag{13}
\]
Thus (11) implies equal powers, hence \(OM^2-R^2=ON^2-R^2\), and therefore \(OM=ON\).

**Open gap:** a concise, independently checkable derivation of (8) from (9),(10) using sine identities. All orientation, denominator, second-intersection, and directed-power requirements are settled.
