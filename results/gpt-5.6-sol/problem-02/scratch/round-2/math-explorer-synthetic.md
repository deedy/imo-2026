  1. **Midpoint second-intersection theorem (strongest new synthetic opening).** Let \(\omega=(AKL)\), let \(D=\omega\cap AB\setminus\{A\}\), \(E=\omega\cap AC\setminus\{A\}\), and introduce the projectively natural cross-intersections
     \[
     U=BK\cap AC,\qquad V=CL\cap AB.
     \]
     Exact numerical experiments in two unrelated scalene configurations indicate the much stronger statement
     \[
     \boxed{D\text{ is the midpoint of }BV,\qquad E\text{ is the midpoint of }CU.}
     \]
     This is deliberately far from the determinant elimination: it seeks two concrete synthetic descriptions of the second intersections of \(\omega\). Once those descriptions are known, the original metric conclusion collapses immediately to the similarity \(\triangle ABU\sim\triangle ACV\), which is already visible from \(\angle KBA=\angle ACL\) and the common angle at \(A\). The exact hard step is proving the two midpoint descriptions directly from the remaining angle equalities. They are not yet proved and must be treated as conjectural, despite excellent numerical agreement.
  2. **Two auxiliary circles / radical-axis architecture.** The same points \(U,V\) expose two rigorous cyclic quadrilaterals:
     \[
     B,L,N,U\text{ are concyclic} \quad(\angle LBK=\angle LNC),
     \]
     \[
     C,K,M,V\text{ are concyclic} \quad(\angle LCK=\angle BMK),
     \]
     with directed-angle care for supplementary representatives. Call these circles \(\Gamma_C\) and \(\Gamma_B\). Also \(\triangle ABU\sim\triangle ACV\), hence
     \[
     AU\cdot AN=AV\cdot AM,
     \]
     so \(A\) lies on the radical axis of \(\Gamma_C,\Gamma_B\). Numerically, the other radical-axis structure is even sharper: the second common point of \(\omega\) and \(\Gamma_B\) lies on \(BK\), and the second common point of \(\omega\) and \(\Gamma_C\) lies on \(CL\). Equivalently,
     \[
     \operatorname{Pow}_{\omega}(B)=BM\cdot BV,\qquad
     \operatorname{Pow}_{\omega}(C)=CN\cdot CU.
     \]
     These equalities are exactly the midpoint descriptions in Opening 1. The whole problem may therefore be a Miquel/radical-axis configuration hidden in the four lines \(AB,AC,BK,CL\). Exact hard step: certify either one of the claimed radical-axis incidences by a directed angle chase or a Miquel-point identification; merely citing the numerical circle pattern is invalid.
  3. **Complete-quadrilateral/projective involution framing.** Regard \(A,U,V,X\) with \(X=BK\cap CL\) as the intersections of the four lines \(AB,AC,BK,CL\). The midpoint maps on \(AB\) and \(AC\) send \((A,B,V)\) and \((A,C,U)\) to \((M,B,D)\) and \((N,C,E)\) if Opening 1 is true. Thus the sought circle is plausibly characterized by a pair of affine midpoint involutions coupled through the similar triangles \(ABU,ACV\). Candidate projective tools are the Miquel theorem for a complete quadrilateral, spiral similarities attached to \(\Gamma_B,\Gamma_C\), or an involution on the two pencils through \(A\). The exact hard step is finding a projectively meaningful statement that forces *midpoints* (an affine notion); a bare projective transformation will not preserve them, so the line at infinity or the factor-2 homotheties about \(M,N\) must be retained.
  4. **Antipode reformulation as a separate synthetic target.** If \(P\) is the antipode of \(A\) on \(\omega\), then \(O\) is the midpoint of \(AP\), and \(OM=ON\) is equivalent to \(PB=PC\). Numerically \(P\) lies on the perpendicular bisector of \(BC\). This avoids powers and second intersections entirely and invites a composition of the three given angle correspondences as spiral similarities. Its exact hard step is scale control: the angle correspondences alone provide rotations, but one must prove the accumulated similarity ratio is \(1\). No convincing synthetic concatenation was found, so this is secondary to Openings 1–2.
