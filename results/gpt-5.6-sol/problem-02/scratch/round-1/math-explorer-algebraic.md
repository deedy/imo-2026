## imo-2026-02
- Distinct openings:
  1. **Equal-power / second-intersection encoding.** Let the circumcircle of \(AKL\) meet \(AB,AC\) again at \(P=pB,Q=qC\) after putting \(A=0\). The conclusion is exactly equality of the powers of the midpoints \(M,N\), hence the scalar identity \(c^2(1-2p)=b^2(1-2q)\), where \(c=AB,b=AC\). This removes the circumcentre entirely and turns the whole problem into finding two line-circle intersection parameters from the angle data. The main hard step is expressing \(p,q\) without generating an unmanageable trigonometric expression.
  2. **Three-angle trigonometric parametrization.** Write \(\alpha=\angle A,\beta=\angle B,\gamma=\angle C\), and \(x=\angle KBA=\angle ACL\), \(y=\angle LBK=\angle LNC\), \(z=\angle LCK=\angle BMK\). The midpoint triangles immediately give
     \[BK=\frac c2\frac{\sin z}{\sin(x+z)},\qquad CL=\frac b2\frac{\sin y}{\sin(x+y)}.\]
     Comparing these with triangles \(BKC\) and \(BLC\) gives two separated constraints
     \[\frac c2\frac{\sin z}{\sin(x+z)}=a\frac{\sin(\gamma-x-z)}{\sin(\alpha+2x+z)},\]
     \[\frac b2\frac{\sin y}{\sin(x+y)}=a\frac{\sin(\beta-x-y)}{\sin(\alpha+2x+y)}.\]
     This is a viable whole-problem analytic route: derive the circle target as a trigonometric identity and seek factorization by these two constraints. Its hard step is the final factorization, not existence/signs; the interior hypotheses supply \(0<x<\min(\beta,\gamma)\), \(0<y<\beta-x\), \(0<z<\gamma-x\).
  3. **Oblique affine coordinates and a linear circle coefficient.** Use the basis \((B,C)\): a point is \(X=rB+sC\). Every circle through \(A=0\) has equation
     \[c^2r^2+b^2s^2+2bc\cos\alpha\,rs-\lambda r-\mu s=0.\]
     Substitution of \(K,L\) determines \(\lambda,\mu\) by a \(2\times2\) linear system. Since \(M=(1/2,0),N=(0,1/2)\), the desired equality becomes the single linear coefficient relation
     \[\lambda-\mu=\frac{c^2-b^2}{2}.\]
     The angle conditions can be encoded as determinant/dot-product or tangent relations for the affine coordinates of \(K,L\). This route is promising because the conclusion is linear even though the circle equation is quadratic. The hard step is choosing angle-coordinate variables that preserve this linearity; raw Cartesian coordinates likely obscure it.
  4. **Complex/rotation encoding as a genuinely algebraic fallback.** Normalize \(A=0,B=1,C=w\). Describe the rays \(BK,BL,CL,CK\) by positive real multiples of rotations through \(x,x+y,x,x+z\); the two midpoint-angle conditions then become real-ratio equations. The circle through \(0,K,L\) has equation \(|z|^2-\bar u z-u\bar z=0\), and the target is one real-linear equation in \(u\). Tangent-half-angle variables make all constraints polynomial and permit resultant/factor probing. This may reveal a low-degree hidden identity, but a blind elimination is likely too large; it should only be used after the equal-power target has reduced the needed output to one scalar.
- Candidate technique(s): Power-of-a-point linearization of circumcentre distances; sine-law encoding of angle constraints; oblique affine/barycentric coordinates; complex rotations and tangent-half-angle polynomialization. A useful exact linearization is: if the circle \((AKL)\) is \(|X|^2-U\cdot X=0\), then \(U=2O\), and \(OM=ON\) is equivalent to \(U\cdot(C-B)=(b^2-c^2)/2\).
- Cheap-kill candidates: **Equal powers of the midpoints with respect to \((AKL)\)** is the strongest cheap reduction: \(OM=ON\iff \operatorname{Pow}(M)=\operatorname{Pow}(N)\). It converts the target to a one-dimensional product identity along \(AB,AC\). Also test the isosceles case \(AB=AC\): the equations are symmetric under swapping \(B\leftrightarrow C\), \(K\leftrightarrow L\), and numerically the relevant interior branch has \(y=z\), suggesting the general identity is a weighted deformation of symmetry. No parity or pigeonhole pruning applies.
