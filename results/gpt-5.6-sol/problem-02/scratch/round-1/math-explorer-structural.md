## imo-2026-02
- Problem statement / metadata: **Geometry; proof_only; answer_type none.** “Let $ABC$ be a triangle and let points $M$ and $N$ be the midpoints of sides $AB$ and $AC$, respectively. Let points $K$ and $L$ be chosen inside triangles $BMC$ and $BNC$, respectively, such that $K$ lies inside the angle $LBA$, $L$ lies inside the angle $ACK$, and $\angle KBA = \angle ACL$, $\angle LBK = \angle LNC$, $\angle LCK = \angle BMK$. Let $O$ be the circumcentre of triangle $AKL$. Prove that $OM = ON$.”
- Distinct openings:
  1. **Equal-power / radical-axis framing.** Since $O$ is the centre of the circle $\omega=(AKL)$, $OM=ON$ is exactly $\operatorname{Pow}_\omega(M)=\operatorname{Pow}_\omega(N)$. Thus the true target may be an equal-power identity rather than a direct circumcentre computation. The three cyclically arranged angle equalities are suggestive of spiral similarities or auxiliary cyclic quadrilaterals that could expose those two powers.
  2. **Fixed-locus / deformation invariant.** For fixed $ABC$, the admissible configurations appear numerically to form a one-parameter family, while every resulting circumcentre lies on the one fixed line: the perpendicular bisector of $MN$. This reframes the entire claim as a locus theorem. The family has a natural limiting configuration near $K=M,L=N$ as $\angle KBA\to0$, and the locus phenomenon resembles a family of directly similar triangles whose circumcentres remain collinear. The hard issue is finding the similarity or invariant governing different parameter values; parameter counting alone proves nothing.
  3. **Candidate-centre / linearized coordinate framing.** Put $A$ at the origin and denote the position vectors of $B,C,K,L,O$ by $b,c,k,l,o$. Circumcentrality gives the two linear equations $2o\cdot k=|k|^2$ and $2o\cdot l=|l|^2$, while the desired result is the single linear condition
     $$o\cdot(b-c)=\frac{|b|^2-|c|^2}{4}.$$
     Equivalently, intersect the perpendicular bisector of $AK$ with the fixed perpendicular bisector of $MN$ and test whether that point is also on the perpendicular bisector of $AL$. This is a potentially controlled analytic route; its hard step is translating all three *oriented* angle/interiority conditions without introducing extraneous branches.
  4. **Angle-parameter / trigonometric encoding.** Let $x=\angle KBA=\angle ACL$, $y=\angle LBK=\angle LNC$, and $z=\angle LCK=\angle BMK$. The local triangles immediately give
     $$BK=\frac{AB}{2}\frac{\sin z}{\sin(x+z)},\quad MK=\frac{AB}{2}\frac{\sin x}{\sin(x+z)},$$
     $$CL=\frac{AC}{2}\frac{\sin y}{\sin(x+y)},\quad NL=\frac{AC}{2}\frac{\sin x}{\sin(x+y)}.$$
     These formulas expose the midpoint factors and make the remaining incidence constraints explicit. They may feed either a sine-law proof of equal powers or a complex/vector factorization. The danger is a large trigonometric elimination with sign errors; the inside hypotheses should be used to keep $x,y,z$ in the intended branches.
  5. **Homothety-at-$A$ reformulation.** Under the dilation of ratio $2$ centred at $A$, $M,N$ go to $B,C$ and $O$ goes to a point $O'$. Hence $OM=ON$ is equivalent to $O'B=O'C$. Also $O'$ is the circumcentre of the dilated triangle with vertices $A,2K,2L$. This aligns the conclusion directly with the original vertices $B,C$ and may make the midpoint-angle data structural rather than incidental. The missing observation is a clean relation between the rays through $M,N$ and the dilated points.
