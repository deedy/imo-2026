## imo-2026-04
- Distinct openings:
  - **Affine angle-simplex / lattice opening.** Encode a triangle only by its ordered angle triple `(a,b,c)` with `a+b+c=180°`. Cutting from the vertex of angle `a`, with the cut making angle `x` from the side adjacent to `b`, offers Shan-Yu the two states `(x,b,a+c-x)` and `(a-x,c,b+x)`. After normalizing by `θ`, the sole global parameter is `s=180°/θ`; the transition is affine and points strongly to the integer lattice in the simplex.
  - **Backward-attractor opening.** Regard the terminal set as the three line segments where one coordinate is `1` after normalization. The one-move predecessor condition is that some choice of vertex and `x` sends both children into the current winning set. Finite-grid attractor computations suggest that the entire positive simplex is attracted exactly when `s` is an integer; the natural layers appear to be triangles having an angle equal to a positive integer, ranked by that integer.
  - **Coset-coloring / adversarial opening.** For nonintegral `s`, color an angle “safe” when it is not an integer multiple of `θ`. The transition formulas show a striking local obstruction: from a state with all three angles safe, both offered children cannot simultaneously acquire an integer-multiple angle unless one old angle was already an integer multiple or `s` itself is integral. This looks like a robust Shan-Yu invariant, substantially different from trying to defeat a proposed Mulan strategy move-by-move.
  - **Interval-crossing / extremal opening.** When `s` is integral but no current angle is an integer, the affine formulas reduce the search for a productive cut to finding an integer strictly inside an interval of the form `(b,b+a)`. Small examples and the sum constraint strongly indicate that some cyclicly ordered pair always supplies such an interval; this is the cheap structural bridge from an arbitrary triangle to the integer-angle layers.
  - **Nested-subdivision-tree opening.** A full play is a nested chain in a binary triangle-subdivision tree. Integer-multiple angles behave like discrete depth labels under a fan cut, while a noninteger fractional-part coloring can select a safe branch at every node. This reframes finite-time guarantee as well-foundedness of the integer-labeled subtree versus existence of an infinite safe branch.
- Candidate technique(s): Normalize angles by `θ`; affine state-space reformulation; integer/fractional-part invariant; backward induction on an integer-valued angle rank; extremal choice of a cyclic interval crossing an integer; adversarial branch selection.
- Cheap-kill candidates: The transition algebra is the main cheap kill. If both children of one cut contain integer-multiple angles while the parent has none, the possible sources of those integer angles can be paired explicitly; all pairings are incompatible except the pairing whose two new angles sum to the total `s`, forcing `s` integral. Conversely, for integral `s`, first try the elementary fact that among three positive nonintegers summing to an integer, one oriented interval `(b,b+a)` contains an integer. No metric information about side lengths or the location of `P` is needed beyond `0<x<a`.
