## imo-2026-01

arithmetic-product-content: new
Target: Prove that every sequence of legal moves ends after finitely many moves with exactly one entry greater than 1, and that this entry is independent of the sequence (indeed, give its prime-exponent formula).
Technique: Direct arithmetic invariant/monovariant; integer descent on a weighted board product, followed by divisor analysis with prime-adic valuations. This adapts the discrete-norm termination move of `aimo-0440` and the valuation-sum philosophy of `aimo-0236`, while proving the present identities from scratch.
Skeleton:
  1. For a board state define the number `k` of nonunit entries, its ordinary product `P`, and the positive integer `Psi=2^k P` — by direct construction of an integer-valued monovariant.
  2. If a selected pair has `g=gcd(m,n)>1`, show its replacement product is `mn/g`, so `P` loses a factor `g`; meanwhile `k` cannot increase, hence `Psi` strictly falls — by `gcd(m,n)lcm(m,n)=mn` and case distinction between `m=n` and `m!=n`.
  3. If `g=1`, the outputs are `1,mn`, so `P` is unchanged and `k` falls by exactly one; hence `Psi` again strictly falls — by coprimality.
  4. Conclude that no infinite play exists because a strictly decreasing sequence of positive integers is finite; when play stops, at most one entry exceeds 1 — by well-ordering and the definition of a legal move.
  5. For each prime `p` appearing on the initial board, set `d_p=gcd_i v_p(a_i)` (with all 2026 exponents included). Prove `d_p` is move-invariant — by translating the selected exponents `(r,s)` to `(min(r,s),|r-s|)` and using `gcd(r,s)=gcd(min(r,s),|r-s|)`.
  6. Since some initial prime has positive `d_p`, a stopped board cannot consist only of ones; combine with Step 4 to obtain exactly one nonunit `M` — by preservation of the corresponding coordinate gcd.
  7. At a board with entries `1,...,1,M`, the invariant for prime `p` equals `v_p(M)`; therefore `M=product_p p^{d_p}`, proving choice-independence — by unique factorization.
Key lemmas (claim + the one-line mechanism that makes it true):
  - `Psi=2^k P` decreases in every move — because a nontrivial gcd divides `P` away, whereas in the coprime case `P` stays fixed but two nonunits become one.
  - Every prime-coordinate content `gcd_i v_p(a_i)` is invariant — because replacing two entries by `min(r,s)` and `|r-s|` is one subtractive Euclidean step and preserves their gcd even when one exponent is zero.
  - The stopped board is not all ones — because any prime occurring initially has a positive gcd among its exponent list, and that invariant would be zero on an all-zero exponent column.
Open gaps: The builder must write the exact inequalities for `Psi`, explicitly handle `m=n`, justify the finite prime range, and fully prove the coordinate-gcd replacement identity.
Cases to cover: `gcd(m,n)=1`; `gcd(m,n)>1` with `m=n`; `gcd(m,n)>1` with `m!=n`.
Watch out for: The replacement product is `mn/g`, not `mn` and not `mn/g^2`; ordinary product alone is not strict in the coprime case. Zeros in a valuation column must be included in its gcd.

valuation-vector-normal-form: new
Target: Prove both termination with exactly one nonunit and schedule-independent value by showing that the board rewrite system has a forced irreducible normal form.
Technique: Reformulation in the free commutative monoid of prime-exponent vectors; `L^1` monovariant plus invariant coordinate-generated subgroups. This adapts the coordinate-signature move of `aimo-0750` and the “monovariant plus terminal characterization” separation of `aimo-0295`.
Skeleton:
  1. Let `P` be the finite set of primes dividing the initial product and encode each board entry as `x_i=(v_p(a_i))_{p in P}` in `N^P`; prove no new prime ever appears — by the formulas for valuations of gcd and lcm.
  2. Translate a move to `(x,y)->(x wedge y, |x-y|)`, where both operations are coordinatewise — by `v_p(gcd)=min` and `v_p(lcm)=max`.
  3. Define `E=sum_i ||x_i||_1` and `k=#{i:x_i!=0}`. Show `Phi=E+k` strictly decreases: if the chosen supports overlap then `E` drops by the positive overlap norm; if they are disjoint then `E` is fixed and the two nonzero vectors become `0,x+y`, lowering `k` — by the identity `min(r,s)+|r-s|=max(r,s)` in every coordinate.
  4. Infer strong normalization for every schedule, and characterize irreducibility as having at most one nonzero vector — by well-ordering and equivalence of nonzero vectors with integers greater than 1.
  5. For each coordinate `p`, let `H_p` be the subgroup of `Z` generated by the column entries. Prove a move leaves `H_p` unchanged in both directions — because `min(r,s)` and `|r-s|` are integer combinations of `r,s`, while after ordering `r>=s`, one recovers `r=s+(r-s)`.
  6. The positive generator `d_p` of `H_p` is therefore invariant. At an irreducible board, its unique nonzero vector must be `(d_p)_{p in P}` — by the classification of subgroups of `Z` and because `P` contains only initially occurring primes, so each `d_p>0`.
  7. Translate that vector back to `M=product_{p in P}p^{d_p}` and conclude both parts — by unique factorization.
Key lemmas (claim + the one-line mechanism that makes it true):
  - `Phi=E+k` is strict for every synchronized vector move — because overlap removes exactly `sum_p min(x_p,y_p)` units of `L^1` mass, while zero overlap merges two occupied positions.
  - Each coordinate-generated subgroup is invariant — because the Euclidean replacement and its inverse generate the same subgroup of `Z`.
  - There is exactly one nonzero vector at termination — because irreducibility gives at most one and every coordinate in the finite prime universe has nonzero invariant generator.
Open gaps: The builder must carefully prove the reverse subgroup inclusion without assuming a common ordering across different primes, and make clear that the argument is coordinate-by-coordinate so the ordering may vary with `p`.
Cases to cover: Chosen vectors with overlapping support; chosen vectors with disjoint support; coordinate cases `r>=s` and `s>=r`.
Watch out for: Do not claim the entire vector lattice generated by the board is invariant; only each coordinate column subgroup is needed and is plainly invariant. Primewise orderings need not be synchronized.

colored-stack-euclidean: new
Target: Prove the entire blackboard claim via a finite colored-token process, including the explicit schedule-independent terminal stack and hence terminal integer.
Technique: Combinatorial encoding by colored stacks, occupied-box descent, and Euclidean common-step invariants.
Skeleton:
  1. Give every prime dividing the initial numbers a color, and at place `i` put `v_p(a_i)` tokens of color `p`; identify unit entries with empty places — by unique factorization.
  2. Describe a move geometrically: for each color, two stack heights `(r,s)` are replaced by their overlap `min(r,s)` and their unmatched height `|r-s|` — by valuation formulas.
  3. Let `T` be total token count and `B` the number of nonempty places. Show `T+B` decreases: common-colored overlap makes `T` fall, while absence of every overlap makes one place empty and consolidates all tokens in the other — by a complete overlap/no-overlap split.
  4. Hence every play stops, and it stops exactly when fewer than two boxes are occupied — by discrete descent and the move rule.
  5. For each color, prove the gcd of all stack heights is preserved by every local overlap/difference replacement — by the elementary Euclidean gcd identity.
  6. Use a color occurring initially to rule out zero occupied boxes, so the terminal state has exactly one occupied box — by positivity of that color's gcd invariant.
  7. Read each terminal stack height from its invariant gcd and reconstruct `M`; this makes the answer independent of play — by unique factorization.
Key lemmas (claim + the one-line mechanism that makes it true):
  - Token count plus occupied boxes strictly decreases — because either some selected-color stacks overlap, removing tokens, or all selected supports are disjoint, merging two occupied boxes into one.
  - A color's common stack step is invariant — because overlap and difference are the subtractive Euclidean algorithm on the two affected heights.
Open gaps: Formalize simultaneous colors without pictorial hand-waving, quantify the token-count loss, and verify that an output place cannot create an additional occupied box.
Cases to cover: At least one common color; completely disjoint color supports; equal stack heights; zero height in one selected color.
Watch out for: This is presentation-diverse but mathematically correlated with the valuation-vector route; it should be built only if the reviewer values the more intuitive combinatorial exposition.

squarefree-layers-lift: new
Target: Prove both assertions by first solving the set-valued squarefree process and then lifting its normal-form mechanism to arbitrary prime multiplicities.
Technique: Specialize then generalize; intersection/symmetric-difference set dynamics followed by a multiplicity-layer Euclidean lift.
Skeleton:
  1. In the squarefree specialization encode each entry by its set of prime divisors; a move becomes `(A,B)->(A intersection B, A symmetric_difference B)` — by direct prime-membership checking.
  2. Establish termination in the set process using total incidences plus the number of nonempty sets, and identify the terminal set as the union of all initial prime sets — because each color occurs with exponent list in `{0,1}`, whose gcd is 1 if it occurs.
  3. For arbitrary exponents replace sets by finite multisets/stack layers and prove the multiplicity lift: on each color the move is subtractive Euclid, so its terminal multiplicity is the gcd of all initial multiplicities — by induction on a global incidence descent, not by assuming independent schedules.
  4. Use total multiplicity plus occupied places to prove the lifted process terminates under every synchronized schedule — by the same overlap/disjoint-support split.
  5. Conclude exactly one occupied place and reconstruct its integer from all terminal multiplicities — by positivity of each occurring color content and unique factorization.
Key lemmas (claim + the one-line mechanism that makes it true):
  - In the squarefree case the terminal color set is the initial union — because the gcd of a nonzero binary exponent column is 1 and is Euclidean-invariant.
  - The multiplicity lift preserves each color's gcd despite synchronized choices — because invariance is local to the affected two heights and requires no control of the schedule.
Open gaps: Step 3 must be stated and proved without circularly importing the whole valuation-vector normal-form proof; otherwise this is not genuinely separate. The builder must decide whether an induction on total multiplicity yields a clean self-contained lift.
Cases to cover: Squarefree overlap/disjointness; general equal/unequal multiplicities; primes absent from one or more places.
Watch out for: The union is invariant only in the squarefree set process, not as a multiplicity statement. This route is speculative and lower-priority because its lift may merely rename the vector argument.
