## imo-2026-01
- Distinct openings:
  1. **Colored-token / occupied-box process.** Regard each prime factor as a color and each exponent at a blackboard place as a pile of tokens of that color. On two places, every color-pair `(a,b)` changes to `(min(a,b), |a-b|)`. This exposes a choice-independent integer potential: total token count together with the number of nonempty places. It also makes the terminal condition purely combinatorial. This is the cleanest genuinely process/invariant framing.
  2. **Lattice-generator signature.** Encode each place by its vector of prime valuations in `N^P`, where `P` is the finite set of primes initially present. For each fixed coordinate `p`, the additive subgroup of `Z` generated by all `p`-coordinates is a move-invariant; its positive generator is `gcd_i v_p(a_i)`. At a one-place terminal state this signature reads off that place coordinate-by-coordinate. Candidate characterization: `M = product_p p^{gcd_i v_p(a_i)}` (zeros included in the gcd in the usual way). This treats part (b) as uniqueness of a normal form under a complete terminal signature, not as manipulation of gcd/lcm expressions.
  3. **Squarefree set-process first, then multiplicities.** In the squarefree special case, a place is a set of prime-colors and a move replaces two sets by their intersection and symmetric difference. The terminal set is suggested to be the union of all initially occurring colors. The general case is the multiplicity/Euclidean analogue, with each color’s surviving multiplicity governed by the gcd of its initial multiplicities. This opening is intuitive and may provide a short exposition before passing to valuation piles.
  4. **Prime-by-prime Euclidean chips.** For a fixed prime, positive exponent piles undergo the subtractive Euclidean operation `(a,b) -> (min(a,b), |a-b|)`. Although different primes are coupled by the requirement that the same two places be selected, termination can be monitored globally rather than trying to schedule each coordinate separately. This avoids the likely dead end of proving every prime-coordinate process terminates independently under an adversarial coupled schedule.
- Candidate technique(s): Re-encode integers as finite colored valuation piles; use a strict integer monovariant valid for every move; use the coordinatewise generated subgroup/gcd as the terminal signature. A particularly cheap candidate potential is `Phi = (sum over all places of Omega(value)) + (# places carrying a value > 1)`: evidence and the valuation rule indicate every legal move lowers `Phi` by at least one. The likely terminal value is `M = product_p p^{gcd_i v_p(a_i)}`.
- Cheap-kill candidates: The token-count plus occupied-place potential appears to settle finiteness without any extremal choice or heavy estimate. For a coprime selected pair, token count is unchanged but the number of occupied places falls by one; for a non-coprime pair, token count falls and occupied-place count does not rise. For choice-independence, take the gcd of all multiplicities of each prime-color; the two-coordinate subtractive move preserves that gcd. No parity or pigeonhole obstruction seems needed.

- Dead ends (do not retry): No recorded failed approaches. Conceptually avoid (i) tracking only the product of all board entries, since it is unchanged on coprime moves and therefore does not prove termination; (ii) tracking only the number of nonunits, since it can stay constant on non-coprime moves; and (iii) trying to prove each prime coordinate terminates in isolation, because a legal move is selected using whole integers and may repeatedly act trivially on a given coordinate. The combined potential removes this coupling issue.
- Small-case / intuition notes: **Computational evidence, not proof:** for random lists of lengths 2 through 8 with entries below 100, every tested random play terminated and always produced `product_p p^{gcd_i v_p(a_i)}`. Examples: `[12,18] -> 6`, `[6,10] -> 30`, `[6,35] -> 210`, `[4,8,16] -> 2`, `[12,75,98] -> 7350`, and `[2,3,5,7] -> 210`, with move counts varying but terminal values fixed. The squarefree case behaves exactly as intersection/symmetric-difference of prime sets. The main presentation gap for an eventual proof is not mathematical but expository: define the finite prime-color universe and the zero-exponent convention cleanly, and verify that the proposed potential decreases in both `gcd=1` and `gcd>1` cases without overlooking the equal-input case.
