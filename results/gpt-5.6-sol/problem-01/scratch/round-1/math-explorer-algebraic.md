## imo-2026-01
- Distinct openings:
  1. **Prime-exponent vector / free commutative monoid framing.** Encode each board integer as its finite-support vector \((v_p(n))_p\in\mathbb N^{(\mathcal P)}\). A move on vectors \(x,y\) is coordinatewise \((x,y)\mapsto (\min(x,y),|x-y|)\). Thus the apparent gcd/lcm process is a simultaneous subtractive-Euclidean operation in every prime coordinate. This is the cleanest genuinely algebraic route.
  2. **Term-rewriting / normal-form framing.** Regard a board as a multiset of nonzero exponent vectors, with zero vectors representing 1. The question becomes strong normalization (every reduction sequence terminates) plus uniqueness of the irreducible normal form. The likely normal form is one nonzero vector whose \(p\)-coordinate is the gcd of all initial \(p\)-coordinates.
  3. **Scalar arithmetic potential framing.** Let \(\Omega(n)=\sum_p v_p(n)\) and let \(k\) be the number of entries greater than 1. For a selected pair, the total \(\Omega\)-mass drops by exactly \(\Omega(\gcd(m,n))\); if that drop is zero, the pair is coprime and the two nontrivial entries become \(1,mn\), so \(k\) drops. This suggests either the lexicographic pair \((\sum\Omega,k)\) or the single integer \(\sum\Omega+k\) as an immediate strong-normalization measure.
  4. **Coordinate-content invariant.** For every prime \(p\), the quantity \(d_p=\gcd_i v_p(a_i)\) is preserved because \(\gcd(r,s)=\gcd(\min(r,s),|r-s|)\). If termination leaves one nontrivial entry, its exponent vector is forced to be \((d_p)_p\), suggesting \(M=\prod_p p^{d_p}\). This invariant is not the ordinary gcd or product of the board; e.g. \((6,10,15)\) appears to finish at \(30\).
- Candidate technique(s): Prime-adic exponent-vector encoding; subtractive Euclidean algorithm coordinatewise; invariant/monovariant for a terminating rewrite system; unique-normal-form identification by coordinatewise gcd (“content”). The algebraic heart is the identity \((r,s)\mapsto(\min(r,s),|r-s|)\), not casework on the integers themselves.
- Cheap-kill candidates: The combined potential \(\Phi=\sum_i\Omega(a_i)+\#\{i:a_i>1\}\) appears to decrease on every legal move: common-prime moves lose \(\Omega(\gcd)\ge1\), while coprime moves preserve \(\Omega\)-mass but remove one nontrivial entry. Also, two inputs greater than 1 cannot both be replaced by 1, so the nontrivial-entry count never reaches zero. This likely disposes of part (a) before any heavy machinery.
