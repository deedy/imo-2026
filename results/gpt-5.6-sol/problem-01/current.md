## Current best
Every possible play terminates with exactly one entry greater than `1`. If the initial entries are `a_1,\ldots,a_{2026}`, that terminal entry is
\[
M=\prod_{p\mid a_1\cdots a_{2026}}p^{\gcd(v_p(a_1),\ldots,v_p(a_{2026}))},
\]
so it is independent of all choices.

## Full proof
Let the initial entries be `a_1,\ldots,a_{2026}`. We use the **invariant/monovariant method** and **divisor analysis prime by prime**, as listed in the knowledge base.

At any stage, let `P` be the product of all entries and let `k` be the number of entries greater than `1`. We claim that the positive integer
\[
\Psi=2^kP
\]
strictly decreases after every legal move.

Suppose `m,n>1` are selected and put `g=\gcd(m,n)`. By the gcd-lcm product identity,
\[
\gcd(m,n)\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)=mn.
\]
The product of the two replacement entries is therefore
\[
g\cdot\frac{\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)}g=\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)=\frac{mn}{g},
\]
so the new board product is `P'=P/g`.

If `g=1`, the outputs are `1,mn`, so `k'=k-1` and `\Psi'=\Psi/2`. If `g>1` and `m=n`, the outputs are `m,1`, so `k'=k-1` and `\Psi'=\Psi/(2m)`. Finally, if `g>1` and `m\ne n`, both outputs exceed `1`: the second could equal `1` only if `\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)=g`, which would force `m=g=n`. Thus `k'=k` and `\Psi'=\Psi/g\le\Psi/2`. These cases are disjoint and exhaustive, and in every case `\Psi` strictly decreases. Hence no infinite sequence of moves exists.

A move is possible exactly when at least two entries exceed `1`; consequently every terminal board has at most one such entry.

It remains to show that there is exactly one and that its value is fixed. Let
\[
S=\{p:p\text{ is prime and }p\mid a_1\cdots a_{2026}\}.
\]
This finite set is nonempty because every initial entry exceeds `1`. For each `p\in S`, define
\[
d_p=\gcd(v_p(a_1),\ldots,v_p(a_{2026})),
\]
including zero valuations. Since at least one valuation in this column is positive, `d_p>0`.

Fix a prime `p`. If the selected entries have valuations `r=v_p(m)` and `s=v_p(n)`, the standard valuation formulas, consequences of the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, show that the replacement valuations are
\[
(\min(r,s),\ |r-s|).
\]
These two pairs have the same gcd. Indeed, if `r\ge s`, then an integer divides both `r,s` exactly when it divides both `s,r-s`; the case `s\ge r` is symmetric. This includes equality and zero valuations. Adjoining all unchanged valuations proves that the gcd of the complete `p`-exponent column is invariant under a move.

A terminal board cannot consist entirely of ones: for any `p\in S`, its terminal exponent-column gcd would then be `0`, contradicting the invariant positive value `d_p`. Therefore every terminal board has exactly one entry `M>1`, proving part (a).

At such a board, the `p`-exponent column consists of zeros except for `v_p(M)`. Its gcd is therefore `v_p(M)`, and invariance gives
\[
v_p(M)=d_p\qquad(p\in S).
\]
No prime outside `S` can appear, because its valuations begin all zero and the update sends `(0,0)` to `(0,0)`. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic now yields
\[
M=\prod_{p\in S}p^{d_p}
 =\prod_{p\mid a_1\cdots a_{2026}}p^{\gcd(v_p(a_1),\ldots,v_p(a_{2026}))}.
\]
This depends only on the initial entries and not on the choices of moves, proving part (b).
