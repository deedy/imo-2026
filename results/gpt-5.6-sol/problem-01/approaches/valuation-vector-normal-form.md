## Current best
Every play terminates at the forced normal form
\[
(1,\ldots,1,M),\qquad
M=\prod_{p\mid a_1\cdots a_{2026}}p^{\gcd(v_p(a_1),\ldots,v_p(a_{2026}))},
\]
up to the position of `M`. In particular, exactly one terminal entry is greater than `1`, and its value is independent of all choices.

## Full proof
Let the initial entries be `a_1,\ldots,a_{2026}`. We use the **invariant/monovariant method** from the knowledge base, after a prime-exponent reformulation.

Let `P` be the set of primes dividing the finite positive integer
\[
A=a_1a_2\cdots a_{2026}.
\]
By the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, `P` is finite. It is nonempty because every initial entry is greater than `1`.

For every entry `a` currently on the board, associate its exponent vector
\[
x(a)=(v_p(a))_{p\in P}\in\mathbb N^P.
\]
We first verify that these coordinates describe every later entry. For positive integers `m,n` and every prime `p`, the standard prime-valuation formulas give
\[
v_p(\gcd(m,n))=\min(v_p(m),v_p(n))
\]
and
\[
v_p(\operatorname{lcm}(m,n))=\max(v_p(m),v_p(n)).
\]
Consequently,
\[
v_p\!\left(\frac{\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)}{\gcd(m,n)}\right)
 =\max(v_p(m),v_p(n))-\min(v_p(m),v_p(n))
 =|v_p(m)-v_p(n)|.
\]
If a prime `q` is not in `P`, both selected entries have `q`-valuation zero initially and, inductively, both outputs also have `q`-valuation zero. Thus no prime outside `P` ever appears.

It follows that, if the vectors of the two selected entries are `x=(x_p)_{p\in P}` and `y=(y_p)_{p\in P}`, one move replaces them by
\[
u=x\wedge y=(\min(x_p,y_p))_{p\in P},
\qquad
w=|x-y|=(|x_p-y_p|)_{p\in P}.
\]
An entry is greater than `1` exactly when its exponent vector is nonzero. Hence a legal move selects two nonzero vectors.

We now prove that every sequence of moves is finite. For a board state with vectors `x_1,\ldots,x_{2026}`, define
\[
E=\sum_{i=1}^{2026}\|x_i\|_1
  =\sum_{i=1}^{2026}\sum_{p\in P}(x_i)_p,
\qquad
k=\#\{i:x_i\ne0\},
\qquad
\Phi=E+k.
\]
All three quantities are nonnegative integers. Consider a legal move on `x,y`. In one coordinate, writing `r=x_p` and `s=y_p`, the old contribution to `E` is `r+s`, whereas the new contribution is
\[
\min(r,s)+|r-s|=\max(r,s).
\]
The identity follows from `r\ge s`, when the left side is `s+(r-s)=r`, and from `s\ge r`, when it is `r+(s-r)=s`. Therefore the exact loss of `E` is
\[
E_{\rm old}-E_{\rm new}=\sum_{p\in P}\min(x_p,y_p). \tag{1}
\]

There are two disjoint and exhaustive cases.

First, suppose the supports of `x` and `y` overlap. Then `x_p>0` and `y_p>0` for at least one `p`, so the right side of (1) is at least `1`; thus `E` strictly decreases. The two nonzero selected vectors are replaced by only two vectors, so their contribution to `k` cannot exceed its former value `2`. All other vectors are unchanged. Thus `k` does not increase, and consequently `\Phi` strictly decreases.

Second, suppose the supports are disjoint. Then `\min(x_p,y_p)=0` for every `p`, so (1) says that `E` is unchanged. Coordinatewise, `u=0`, while disjointness gives `w=|x-y|=x+y`. Since `x` and `y` were both nonzero, `x+y` is nonzero. The two occupied positions are therefore replaced by exactly one occupied position, so `k` decreases by exactly `1`. Again `\Phi` strictly decreases.

Thus every legal move strictly decreases the nonnegative integer `\Phi`. By the well-ordering principle, or discrete descent, there cannot be infinitely many legal moves. Every possible schedule therefore terminates.

A board state admits a legal move if and only if it has at least two nonzero exponent vectors, because legal moves select precisely two entries greater than `1` in different positions. Hence a terminal state has at most one nonzero vector. It remains to prove both that it has one and that this vector is forced.

Fix a prime `p\in P`. For a board state define the additive subgroup
\[
H_p=\langle (x_1)_p,\ldots,(x_{2026})_p\rangle_{\mathbb Z}
\]
of `\mathbb Z`. We prove that `H_p` is invariant under every move. Only two generators change, so write their `p`-coordinates as `r,s\in\mathbb N`; they are replaced by
\[
a=\min(r,s),\qquad b=|r-s|.
\]
We show
\[
\langle r,s\rangle_{\mathbb Z}=\langle a,b\rangle_{\mathbb Z}. \tag{2}
\]
This argument is coordinate-by-coordinate; the ordering chosen here may be different for different primes, and no common ordering of the full vectors is being assumed.

If `r\ge s`, then `a=s` and `b=r-s`. Thus `a=s` and `b=r-s` are integer linear combinations of `r,s`, proving `\langle a,b\rangle\subseteq\langle r,s\rangle`; conversely, `s=a` and `r=a+b`, proving `\langle r,s\rangle\subseteq\langle a,b\rangle`.
If `s\ge r`, then `a=r` and `b=s-r`. Thus `a=r` and `b=s-r` are integer linear combinations of `r,s`, while `r=a` and `s=a+b` are integer linear combinations of `a,b`. The two inclusions again follow. This also covers `r=s`, for which `a=r=s` and `b=0`. Therefore (2) always holds. Adding the unchanged column entries to the generating sets on both sides of (2) proves that the whole subgroup `H_p` is unchanged by the move.

Every nonzero subgroup of `\mathbb Z` has a unique positive generator. Let `d_p` be the positive generator of the initial subgroup `H_p`. This number exists because `p\in P`, so at least one initial `p`-valuation is positive and `H_p\ne\{0\}`. Equivalently,
\[
d_p=\gcd\bigl(v_p(a_1),\ldots,v_p(a_{2026})\bigr),
\]
where zero valuations are included; indeed, the subgroup generated by finitely many integers is generated by their greatest common divisor by Bézout's identity. The subgroup invariance proves that its positive generator remains `d_p` throughout the process.

Consider any terminal board. It cannot have all exponent vectors zero: if it did, then for every `p\in P` its terminal subgroup `H_p` would be the zero subgroup, contradicting the invariant equality `H_p=d_p\mathbb Z` with `d_p>0`. Since a terminal board has at most one nonzero vector, it therefore has exactly one; call it `z=(z_p)_{p\in P}`.

For each fixed `p\in P`, the terminal `p`-column consists of zeros except for `z_p`. Hence its generated subgroup is `z_p\mathbb Z`. Invariance gives
\[
z_p\mathbb Z=d_p\mathbb Z.
\]
Both `z_p` and `d_p` are nonnegative, and `d_p>0`; equality of these subgroups forces `z_p=d_p`, because their least positive elements are equal. Thus the unique terminal nonzero vector is forced to be
\[
z=(d_p)_{p\in P}.
\]
By the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, the corresponding terminal integer is
\[
M=\prod_{p\in P}p^{d_p}
 =\prod_{p\mid a_1\cdots a_{2026}}
 p^{\gcd(v_p(a_1),\ldots,v_p(a_{2026}))}.
\]
Every `d_p` is positive, so `M>1`. We have proved that every play ends after finitely many moves with exactly this one entry greater than `1`; since the displayed formula depends only on the initial entries, its value is independent of all choices made by Confucius. ∎

## Promotable lemmas
- **Coordinate-generated subgroup invariance under the gcd/quotient rewrite.** Statement: for a finite list of nonnegative exponent vectors, replacing two vectors `x,y` by `x\wedge y,|x-y|` preserves, for every coordinate `p`, the subgroup of `\mathbb Z` generated by all entries in the `p`-column. Proved in the `H_p` portion of the Full proof by establishing both subgroup inclusions separately in the cases `x_p\ge y_p` and `y_p\ge x_p`.
- **Strong normalization of the exponent-vector rewrite.** Statement: on a finite list of nonnegative vectors, whenever moves are restricted to two nonzero vectors and replace them by `x\wedge y,|x-y|`, the integer `\sum_i\|x_i\|_1+\#\{i:x_i\ne0\}` strictly decreases. Proved in the `E,k,\Phi` portion of the Full proof by the exhaustive overlap/disjoint-support split.
