# The support family is self-dual

## Statement
Let $A=\{a_1,a_2,\ldots\}$ be generated by the greedy rule, and let $\operatorname{supp}(m)$ denote the set of prime divisors of $m$. For every nonempty finite set $S$ of primes, all integers $m\ge a_1$ with $\operatorname{supp}(m)=S$ have the same accepted/rejected status. If $\mathcal F$ is the family of supports having accepted status, then
\[
 S\in\mathcal F\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad S\cap T\ne\varnothing\text{ for every }T\in\mathcal F.
\]
Consequently, if $\mathcal C$ consists of the inclusion-minimal members of $\mathcal F$, then $\mathcal C$ is an intersecting antichain, every member of $\mathcal F$ contains a member of $\mathcal C$, and every finite set meeting all members of $\mathcal C$ contains a member of $\mathcal C$.

## Proof
The recurrence is equivalent to scanning the integers greater than $a_1$ in increasing order, accepting an integer exactly when it is noncoprime to every integer accepted earlier. In particular, accepted integers are pairwise noncoprime, while every rejected integer has a smaller accepted integer coprime to it.

Suppose $x<y$, both are at least $a_1$, and $\operatorname{supp}(x)=\operatorname{supp}(y)$. If $x$ is rejected, a smaller accepted integer coprime to $x$ is also coprime to $y$, so $y$ is rejected. If $x$ is accepted, every accepted integer smaller than $y$ is noncoprime to $x$ and hence, because $x$ and $y$ have the same prime support, is noncoprime to $y$. Thus $y$ is accepted. This proves support invariance.

Every nonempty finite prime set is the support of some integer at least $a_1$, so $\mathcal F$ is well-defined. If $S\in\mathcal F$, pairwise noncoprimality shows that $S$ meets every $T\in\mathcal F$. Conversely, suppose $S$ meets every member of $\mathcal F$ and choose an integer $x\ge a_1$ with support $S$. Every accepted integer smaller than $x$ has support in $\mathcal F$, and hence is noncoprime to $x$. The scanning rule therefore accepts $x$, so $S\in\mathcal F$.

Every $S\in\mathcal F$ contains an inclusion-minimal member of $\mathcal F$, because $S$ is finite. Hence $\mathcal C$ is an antichain generating $\mathcal F$ upward. It is intersecting because $\mathcal F$ is intersecting. Finally, if a finite set $R$ meets every member of $\mathcal C$, it meets every member of $\mathcal F$, since every member of $\mathcal F$ contains one from $\mathcal C$. The displayed equivalence gives $R\in\mathcal F$, and therefore $R$ contains a member of $\mathcal C$.
