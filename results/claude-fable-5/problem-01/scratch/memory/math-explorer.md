ALWAYS: for "repeatedly merge/replace a pair" processes (gcd/lcm, or similar pairwise ops), immediately reduce to the per-prime exponent dynamic via v_p, then check whether a simple algebraic identity (e.g. gcd(min(a,b),|a-b|)=gcd(a,b)) makes some global per-prime quantity (gcd of the whole exponent multiset) invariant — this often solves an "independent of choices" (part b) sub-question outright with no casework (round 1, imo-2026-01).
ALWAYS: numerically simulate the actual process (small random instances, sympy factorint) to confirm an invariant/monovariant conjecture before writing it into the report — cheap and catches sign/off-by-one errors in the exponent-map derivation (round 1, imo-2026-01).
NEVER: try to prove termination of a multi-prime coupled process via separate per-prime arguments combined naively — moves touch all primes of the chosen pair simultaneously, so termination needs one global monovariant (e.g. total Ω(n_i) summed over the whole board, refined lexicographically by a secondary counter) rather than per-prime counters (round 1, imo-2026-01).
ALWAYS: for "blackboard process" invariant problems, try computing per-prime p-adic valuation statistics (gcd/min/max of v_p across entries) as invariant candidates via direct simulation before reaching for heavy machinery like Newman's lemma/confluence — often an elementary algebraic identity (e.g. gcd(a,b)=gcd(min(a,b),|a-b|)) gives a one-line invariance proof that's stronger and simpler than an abstract-rewriting argument (round 1, imo-2026-01).
NEVER: assume outputs of a gcd/lcm-swap move (gcd(m,n), lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n)) are coprime to each other — false in general (m=4,n=6 gives 2,6, gcd=2) — a tempting but wrong cheap-kill lemma (round 1, imo-2026-01).
