## imo-2026-01

- Distinct openings:
  1. **Product-monovariant termination proof (for part a)** — found via simulation + algebra, essentially closes part (a) rigorously. See "Candidate technique(s)" below for the exact statement.
  2. **Per-prime valuation-gcd invariant (for part b)** — found via simulation + algebra; a direct elementary computation (not confluence/rewriting theory) that proves M is invariant move-by-move. This is a *stronger and more elementary* route than Newman's lemma / abstract confluence, and it fully determines the closed formula for M.
  3. **Confluence / Newman's-lemma framing** — works as a *conceptual* route (local confluence + termination ⇒ unique normal form) but is strictly weaker than opening 2: it would prove *some* well-defined final board exists but doesn't by itself hand you the closed-form value of M; you'd still need essentially the same per-prime gcd computation to identify what that normal form is. Recommend using it only as a secondary/backup framing, not the primary one.
  4. **Whole-board (not just >1 part) confluence** — empirically the *entire* terminal multiset (all the 1's plus the single M) is order-independent, not just M. This is stronger than the problem asks but suggests the invariant is truly complete/exhaustive (no information is lost by the process beyond what the per-prime-gcd captures) — a good sanity check for the outliner but not something that needs to be proved (problem only asks about M).

- Candidate technique(s):
  - **Part (a) — termination.** Monovariant: let `P` = product of all 2026 board entries (including any that have become 1). A move on `m,n` replaces the pair-product `mn` with `gcd(m,n)·lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n) = lcm(m,n) ≤ mn`, so `P` is non-increasing, with equality **iff** `gcd(m,n)=1`. When `gcd(m,n)=1`, the two new entries are `1` and `mn`, so the count `C` of entries `>1` strictly decreases by exactly 1 (verified algebraically: if `gcd(m,n)>1`, both new entries `g=gcd(m,n)>1` and `l/g` are `>1` unless `m=n`, in which case `l/g=1` and `P` strictly drops too — i.e. whenever `C` doesn't strictly decrease, `P` strictly decreases). So the pair `(P, C)` decreases lexicographically in the well-ordered set `ℕ≥1 × {0,...,2026}` on every move ⇒ the process terminates after finitely many moves. Since it stops only when no move is possible, i.e. at most one entry `>1` remains. Matches KB "Invariant / monovariant" entry (knowledge_base.md general proof methods, "find a quantity that never changes (or only moves one way) to prove reachability/unreachability or termination").
  - **Part (b) — invariance / uniqueness of M.** For each prime `p`, let `I_p` = `gcd` of the `p`-adic valuations `v_p(a_i)` over all board entries `a_i` (convention: `gcd(0,x)=x`, so entries not divisible by `p` contribute `0` and don't affect the gcd unless all are 0, giving `I_p=0`). **Claim: `I_p` is invariant under every move.** Proof sketch (elementary, verified by direct algebra — NOT yet fully written as a proof, just the key identity): if the move touches two entries with valuations `a,b` and every other valuation is bundled as `G = gcd of the rest`, then before the move the total gcd is `gcd(G,a,b)`; after, the two valuations become `min(a,b)` and `|a-b|`, and the standard Euclidean-algorithm identity `gcd(a,b) = gcd(min(a,b), |a-b|)` (since for `a≥b`, `gcd(a,b)=gcd(a-b,b)`) gives `gcd(G,a,b) = gcd(G, gcd(a,b)) = gcd(G, gcd(min(a,b),|a-b|)) = gcd(G, min(a,b), |a-b|)`, i.e. the new total gcd equals the old one. So `I_p` is a true invariant, for every prime `p`, throughout the whole process — no confluence machinery is needed. At the terminal state (part a), only one entry `M>1` remains, all others are `1`; then `I_p = gcd(v_p(M), 0,0,...,0) = v_p(M)`. Hence `M = ∏_p p^{I_p}`, a quantity fixed by the **initial** board and **independent of the moves made** — this is exactly part (b), and it also gives the explicit closed-form answer for `M`. This is the strongest, most direct finding of this exploration.
  - Formula: **`M = ∏_p p^{gcd_i(v_p(a_i))}`** where the product is over all primes `p` dividing at least one of the 2026 initial numbers `a_i`, and `gcd_i` is over the (multiset of) `p`-adic valuations of the initial numbers (with the 0-valuation convention above). Confirmed numerically with zero mismatches over 500 random trials × 3 replays each (1500 checks total, sizes 2–7, entries up to 300) — see Small-case notes.

- Cheap-kill candidates:
  - The product monovariant `P` alone (without the count `C`) is *not* strictly decreasing on coprime moves — don't present it alone as a termination proof; must pair with the count-of-entries->1 argument (or an equivalent "number of `1`s is non-decreasing and strictly increases whenever `P` doesn't decrease" framing).
  - Quick parity/pigeonhole pre-check: since gcd(m,n) and lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n) are **coprime** to each other (standard fact: writing `m=g m'`, `n=g n'` with `gcd(m',n')=1`, the new pair is `g` and `m'n'`, and `gcd(g, m'n')` need not be 1 in general — actually check: this is NOT automatically coprime, e.g. m=4,n=6: g=2, l/g=6, gcd(2,6)=2≠1. So this "coprime replacement" cheap-kill does NOT hold in general — flag this as a pitfall, an easy wrong lemma to avoid.)
  - Multiplicity-count sanity check: the total value `Ω(board) = Σ Ω(a_i)` (number of prime factors with multiplicity, i.e. `Σ_p Σ_i v_p(a_i)`) is exactly `log_p`-additive and equals `Ω(P)` where `P` is the product monovariant above — so it's non-increasing too, giving an alternative /equivalent termination monovariant if the outliner prefers working with `Ω` instead of `P` directly (both strictly decrease exactly when `gcd(m,n)>1`).
