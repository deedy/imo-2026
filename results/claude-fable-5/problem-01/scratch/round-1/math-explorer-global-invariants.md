## imo-2026-01

- Distinct openings (within the "global invariant / monovariant" lens):
  1. **Product monovariant for termination.** Let `P` = product of all 2026 board entries. A move replaces `m,n` with `gcd(m,n), lcm(m,n)/gcd(m,n)`, whose product is `lcm(m,n) = mn/gcd(m,n)`. So the new total product is `P_new = P_old / gcd(m,n)` — **`P` is non-increasing, and strictly decreases whenever `gcd(m,n) > 1`.** When `gcd(m,n)=1`, the move sends `(m,n) -> (1, mn)`, i.e. leaves `P` fixed but strictly decreases `N` := (number of board entries `> 1`), since one of the two results becomes exactly 1. Hence the pair `(Ω(P), N)` (with `Ω` = number of prime factors counted with multiplicity) strictly decreases lexicographically at every move — this is a clean, prime-free-at-the-top-level monovariant argument for **termination** (part (a), finiteness). Verified: it is exactly right — never increases, and one of the two coordinates strictly drops each move.
  2. **Exact invariant pinning down M (part (b)), found by direct exponent bookkeeping.** For a fixed prime `p`, write `a=v_p(m), b=v_p(n)`. The move sends the exponent pair `(a,b) -> (min(a,b), |a-b|)`. Elementary fact: `gcd(min(a,b),|a-b|) = gcd(a,b)`. Combined with gcd-associativity `gcd(S ∪ {a,b}) = gcd(gcd(S), gcd(a,b))`, this shows: **for every prime `p`, the quantity `g_p := gcd_{x on board}(v_p(x))` (gcd over the whole 2026-entry multiset, with `v_p(x)=0` counted for numbers not divisible by `p`) is an EXACT invariant of the process**, not just a monovariant — unaffected by which pair is chosen at each step. This was verified computationally to be exact across 30+ random trials (board sizes 2–8, entries up to 60): `M = ∏_p p^{g_p}` matches the simulated terminal value every time (see Small-case section). This single fact essentially answers BOTH parts:
     - **Part (b)** falls out immediately: since terminal board is `{M} ∪ {1,...,1}`, the invariant multiset gcd at prime `p` in the terminal state is `gcd(v_p(M), 0,0,...,0) = v_p(M)`. Since `g_p` never changes, `v_p(M) = g_p` for every `p`, i.e. `M` is *forced* to equal `∏_p p^{g_p}`, a quantity determined purely by the initial board — independent of the choices made.
     - **Part (a)** (ruling out N=0, i.e. all entries collapsing to 1): if the terminal state had every entry equal to 1, every `g_p` would have to be 0. But `g_p = gcd` of a multiset that includes at least one strictly positive value whenever prime `p` divides *some* original board entry (and every original entry is `>1`, so some prime divides some entry) — and `gcd` of a multiset containing a positive number and zeros equals `gcd` of the positive numbers, which is `≥ 1`, never 0. So `g_p ≥ 1` for at least one prime `p` (any prime dividing any original entry), forcing `M = ∏ p^{g_p} > 1`. Combined with the monovariant argument (opening 1) that guarantees termination with `N ≤ 1`, this pins `N` exactly to 1 — completing part (a) without any separate casework.
  3. **Reformulate as coupled subtractive-Euclid processes, one per prime, sharing a pairing schedule.** The move factors completely across primes: which two board slots get paired is chosen once, but the effect on each prime's exponent-vector is independent (`(a,b)->(min,|diff|)`), so the whole process is literally 2026-way, prime-many copies of the classical "slow gcd algorithm" running on a shared randomized pairing schedule. This reframing is what makes invariant #2 obvious and is worth stating explicitly in the proof as the structural reason the per-prime gcd survives arbitrary scheduling.
